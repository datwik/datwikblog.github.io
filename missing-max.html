Missing Max

It's almost 15 years since the first cloud service "Zimki" was developed and made available to the world calling it "Pre-Shaved Yaks". Despite eventually being shutdown 2 years later, the world took notice and now we have more than few dozen big and small cloud service providers made life for the businesses reliant on internet presence magnitude times easier - freeing them from maintaining on-premise hardware and keeping systems up to date.


Source: Flickr

Initially all the clients were of the cloud providers were given static set of resources, such as 2 virtual CPUs and 2GB of RAM. Over time numbers of studies have shown that full capacity is rarely being utilized by the clients, rather resource utilization is variable - hovering around 30-40%, with sudden bursts separated by long valleys of low activity. So from the perspective of the cloud providers tenants cost can be further reduced by providing computing resources on demand, rather than full capacity that may go unused.


Source: Wikimedia

All major cloud providers (AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud Engine) few years ago introduced so called "Burstable" virtual machines (or instances). In short these virtual computers are expected to stay relatively unused for long periods of time, while occasionally serving high load for short period of time (several minutes per day).

For the purposes of this article let's look at one of the smallest instance available from AWS EC2: t3.nano. It has 2 virtual CPUs and 0.5GB RAM. Current price for this instance (as of the date of this article) is $0.0052 per hour, which comes down to rougly $3.8 per month (730 hours in one month). So far, so good.


Source: AWS

What if you have something a bit more resource heavy? With the price of cup of coffee per month, what if we install a web server or database on this machine - it may be a bit slow, but hey it is cheap - why not try it?

AWS has a really good documentation explaining the details about t3 instances. It tells you that baseline performance for these instances is 5% of the CPU, they start in unlimited mode - so they can provide you the requests performance as you need more of. Also a bit below general table of EC2 instance prices it tells you that CPU Credits will be charged at the rate of $0.05 per vCPU. Hmm, what would that mean?

Let's try to unpack the paragraph above. "5% baseline performance" means that you are only able to run 3 minutes every hour at 100% CPU free of charge. Remember t3.nano had 2 virtual CPUs. Every minute beyond that, even if you use only 6% of these CPU you will be charged at $0.095 per hour on top of the base price (1.9 vCPU x $0.05). Since we have 2 virtual CPUs additional charges can accrue up to eye watering $69.35 (730h x 2 x $0.095). So total maximum price in the worst case will be $69.35 + $3.8 = $73.15. This is the "missing max", the price they did not seem to specify in the documentation. In the worst case you will be paying over 19 times more than what is specified as a base price.

Granted there are business cases where you can stay in the baseline price brackets. Usually you will arrive at that after some period of experimentation, and for those cases t3.nano will be an excellent saving opportunity. Other than that, if you just starting a project and do not know the computing requirements that you will need for next little while, it is safer to start with a1.medium, which starts at $18.62 per month and you can push the price down to $10.92 with Reserved Instance pricing for 1 year. On the other side if you are will to pay upwards of $70 per month for an instance, m5.large or a1.xlarge will be much better option with significantly more memory or CPU power.
