Free eBooks Anyone?

On one side there was a Copyright. In 1924 Copyright Act, 1921 came into force in Canada. One of the provisions was extending the term of copyright from 28 years to 50 years after the author passed away. Similar laws were passed in UK and US, protecting the books from entering Public Domain in certain cases up to 95 years. Public Domain concept goes as far back as the ancient Roman Law, as something that could be shared and enjoyed by all citizens.


Source: Crystalinks

On the other side there were eBooks. Idea of one defined as a "talkie" was proposed almost 90 years ago by writer Bob Brown. In the modern terms, it is approximated by audiobooks that we listen on the way to work. In 1949 Angela Ruiz Robles invented Mechanical Encyclopedia, with the aim to reduce amount of books in the student bags (In 2020 with all the gadgets we have, that is still a problem to be tackled!). The book was reportedly operating on spools and air compression, unfortunately did not see much of a light due to depressing economic situation in Spain around those years.


Source: Tedium

In 1971, Michael Hart got access to first account on a classic Xerox Sigma V mainframe with estimated $100,000,000 worth of computing time. Amazed by the price tag, he was determined to earn it back in some other way. Project Gutenberg was the result of his simple idea: "anything that can be entered into a computer can be reproduced indefinitely". Electronic text turned out to be the best fit for that. Available in the vanilla text format, they are very cheap to store, transmit and reproduce. They are also accessible by anyone, regardless of skill, operating system or hardware.


Source: Ebook Friendly

Today Project Gutenberg stores over over 60,000 free eBooks. There is no fee, registration or app needed to use them. You are welcome to download and read every single one of them on your favourite eDevice. If you find it useful, you can give back by digitizing, proofreading and formatting, recording audio books, or reporting errors.
